### Theoretical Foundation

The Black-Scholes model represents a breakthrough in [[financial theory]], providing a mathematical framework for options pricing. The model earned Myron Scholes and Robert Merton the 1997 Nobel Prize in Economics (Fischer Black had passed away before the award).

### Core Assumptions

1. Market Structure:
   - Frictionless markets (no transaction costs)
   - Continuous trading possible
   - Risk-free rate is constant
   - No arbitrage opportunities exist

2. Asset Behavior:
   - Follows [[geometric Brownian motion]]
   - Returns are log-normally distributed
   - Constant volatility over option life

### Key Variables

The model reduces option pricing to five fundamental inputs:

3. Current stock price (S)
4. Option strike price (K)
5. Time to expiration (t)
6. [[Risk-free interest rate (r)]]
7. [[Volatility (σ)]]

### Practical Applications

While real markets violate several Black-Scholes assumptions, the model provides:

8. Theoretical Framework:
   - Understanding of options pricing mechanics
   - Basis for more complex models
   - Foundation for Greeks calculations

9. Risk Management Tools:
   - Delta hedging strategies
   - Portfolio sensitivity analysis
   - Volatility trading frameworks
